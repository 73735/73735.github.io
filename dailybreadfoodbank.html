Daily Bread Food Bank

Information Architecture Redesign.

Probrem

The Daily Bread Food Bank’s website is an integral point of information for the charity’s key supporters. Donors, agencies, and volunteers are key users whose activities enable the Daily Bread to achieve their charitable goals. However, the Daily Bread Food Bank’s website does not currently serve its various users efficiently. The structure and organization of information is overwhelming. Navigation throughout the website also poses a challenge to its users. The result is both a frustrated experience and a misuse of the company’s resources.

Daily Bread Food Bank website before the redesign

Background Research

Content

Our content analysis involved talking to two stakeholders, which give us insight into what information volunteers and companies are looking for when they come to the DBFB website. In addition to speaking with stakeholders we used OnPoint Content Auditor and Content Insight to perform our content audit.

Context

Daily Bread maintain a website that communicates the most important and relevant information to its various partners. However, based on the key users’ goals, there are areas of improvement in the information architecture that will benefit the Daily Bread’s overall business goals: namely, raising funds and donations for families in need.

User
  • Donors: those who support Daily Bread financially and/or with food.
  • Agencies: a network of smaller food banks or community centres in the GTA that provide food and assistance to those in need.
  • Volunteers: people from the community or company employees donate their time to the organization.

User Research

Interviews

We conducted usability testing and semi-structured interview with users who were familiar with the basic Daily Bread’s operation, were somewhat familiar with current information architecture and are comfortable using the internet.

Interview results revealed that the website’s corporate partnership pages are geared towards companies who want to become big donors. Information on ways to contribute is less clear and straightforward for people who would just like to do a nice thing.

A user further remarked that during her experience navigating the website, she hit a few walls. The user feels as though she needs to jump around a lot in order to find relevant information. She feels that pages are clumpy and labels are misleading. She further felt that the labels and headings did not accurately convey the content found under those headings.

Finally, the importance of statistics and research was noted. Although a page dedicated to research is included, the website could better integrate the research the organization have done to provide potential donors with concrete reasons why their help is needed.

Card Sorting

An hybrid card sorting session was conducted to help establish a new labeling and main navigation menu structure. In order to create the cards, we've used the information we gathered from the content analysis.

Tree Test

A tree test was conducted using Optimal Workshop TreeJack tool with eight scenarios.

Information Architecture Schematic Diagram

The fairly consistent card-sorting has lead to the decision to maintain the six main headings in the global navigation menu. However, due to reports of ambiguous and jargonistic labels, the six main headings underwent a task-centric relabelling.

Prototypes

Our research findings helped us create use cases and build personas and scenarios, which guided our sketches and prototypes.
